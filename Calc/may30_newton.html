Newton's Method · Ch.13.4
Calc A2 Final 23 June 2026
Core Formula
\[\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n - [DF(\mathbf{x}_n)]^{-1}\,F(\mathbf{x}_n)\]
2×2 inverse: \(\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix}\)  ·  Differentiable if all partial derivatives are continuous  ·  Linear approx: \(F(\mathbf{x}_0+\mathbf{h})\approx F(\mathbf{x}_0)+[DF(\mathbf{x}_0)]\mathbf{h}\)
Problem 1 Two-Step Iteration & Convergence 12 pts
We want to solve \(F(x,y)=\mathbf{0}\) where
\[F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}x^2 - 2y \\ xy - 1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{x}_0 = (2,\,1).\]
  1. (a) Compute the Jacobian \([DF(x,y)]\) in general, then evaluate at \((2,1)\). Explain why \(F\) is differentiable everywhere. 3 pts
  2. (b) Evaluate \(F(2,1)\) and use linear approximation to estimate \(F(2.01,\,1.01)\). 2 pts
  3. (c) Perform one Newton step to find \(\mathbf{x}_1\). Compute \(F(\mathbf{x}_1)\) and confirm it is closer to \(\mathbf{0}\). 4 pts
  4. (d) Perform a second Newton step from \(\mathbf{x}_1\) to find \(\mathbf{x}_2\). The true solution satisfies \(x^3=2\). How close is \(\mathbf{x}_2\)? 3 pts
(a) Jacobian
\[[DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix}2x & -2 \\ y & x\end{pmatrix}, \qquad [DF(2,1)]=\begin{pmatrix}4 & -2 \\ 1 & 2\end{pmatrix}.\] All entries are polynomials — continuous everywhere — so \(F\) is differentiable everywhere.
(b) Linear Approximation
\(F(2,1)=\begin{pmatrix}4-2\\2-1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}\). With \(\mathbf{h}=(0.01,0.01)^T\):
\[F(2.01,1.01)\approx\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}4&-2\\1&2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0.01\\0.01\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2.02\\1.03\end{pmatrix}.\]
(c) First Newton Step
\(\det[DF(2,1)]=4\cdot2-(-2)\cdot1=10.\) \[[DF(2,1)]^{-1}=\frac{1}{10}\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\-1&4\end{pmatrix}.\] \[[DF]^{-1}F(2,1)=\frac{1}{10}\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\-1&4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}=\frac{1}{10}\begin{pmatrix}6\\2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0.6\\0.2\end{pmatrix}.\]
\[\mathbf{x}_1=(2,1)-(0.6,0.2)=(1.4,\;0.8).\]
Check: \(F(1.4,0.8)=\begin{pmatrix}1.96-1.6\\1.12-1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0.36\\0.12\end{pmatrix}\). Norm \(\approx0.38\), much smaller than \(\|(2,1)\|=\sqrt{5}\) ✓
(d) Second Newton Step
\([DF(1.4,0.8)]=\begin{pmatrix}2.8&-2\\0.8&1.4\end{pmatrix},\quad\det=3.92+1.6=5.52.\) \[[DF]^{-1}=\frac{1}{5.52}\begin{pmatrix}1.4&2\\-0.8&2.8\end{pmatrix}.\] Update vector: \(\frac{1}{5.52}\begin{pmatrix}1.4(0.36)+2(0.12)\\-0.8(0.36)+2.8(0.12)\end{pmatrix}=\frac{1}{5.52}\begin{pmatrix}0.744\\0.048\end{pmatrix}\approx\begin{pmatrix}0.1348\\0.0087\end{pmatrix}.\)
\[\mathbf{x}_2\approx(1.4-0.1348,\;0.8-0.0087)\approx(1.2652,\;0.7913).\]
True solution: \(x=2^{1/3}\approx1.2599,\;y=1/x\approx0.7937\). The error at \(\mathbf{x}_2\) is already \(\approx0.005\) — Newton's method converges quadratically.
Problem 2 3D System — Newton in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) HARD 11 pts
We want to find intersection points of a sphere and two planes. Define \(F:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^3\):
\[F(x,y,z)=\begin{pmatrix}x^2+y^2+z^2-3 \\ x+y-2 \\ y-z\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{x}_0=(2,\,0,\,0).\]
  1. (a) Compute the \(3\times3\) Jacobian \([DF(x,y,z)]\) in general, then evaluate at \((2,0,0)\). 3 pts
  2. (b) Evaluate \(F(2,0,0)\). Is \((2,0,0)\) already a solution? 1 pt
  3. (c) Compute \([DF(2,0,0)]^{-1}\) by row-reducing \([A\,|\,I]\), then perform one Newton step to find \(\mathbf{x}_1\). 5 pts
  4. (d) Find all exact solutions of \(F=\mathbf{0}\). Which one is \(\mathbf{x}_1\) converging toward? 2 pts
(a) 3×3 Jacobian
\[[DF(x,y,z)]=\begin{pmatrix}2x&2y&2z\\1&1&0\\0&1&-1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad [DF(2,0,0)]=\begin{pmatrix}4&0&0\\1&1&0\\0&1&-1\end{pmatrix}.\]
(b) Value at guess
\[F(2,0,0)=\begin{pmatrix}4-3\\2-2\\0-0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix}.\] Not zero, so \((2,0,0)\) is not a solution. Newton must iterate.
(c) Inverse + Newton Step
Row-reduce \([A\,|\,I]\) with \(A=[DF(2,0,0)]\). Since \(A\) is lower triangular with \(\det=4\cdot1\cdot(-1)=-4\): \[A^{-1}=\frac{1}{-4}\begin{pmatrix}-1&0&0\\1&-4&0\\1&-4&4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/4&0&0\\-1/4&1&0\\-1/4&1&-1\end{pmatrix}.\] Update: \(A^{-1}F(2,0,0)=\begin{pmatrix}1/4&0&0\\-1/4&1&0\\-1/4&1&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/4\\-1/4\\-1/4\end{pmatrix}.\)
\[\mathbf{x}_1=(2,0,0)-(1/4,-1/4,-1/4)=(7/4,\;1/4,\;1/4).\]
(d) All Exact Solutions
From \(y=z\) and \(x=2-y\): substitute into sphere: \((2-y)^2+2y^2=3\Rightarrow 3y^2-4y+1=0\Rightarrow(3y-1)(y-1)=0\).
Solutions: \(\bigl(\tfrac{5}{3},\,\tfrac{1}{3},\,\tfrac{1}{3}\bigr)\) and \((1,1,1)\).
\(\mathbf{x}_1=(1.75, 0.25, 0.25)\) is closer to \((\tfrac53, \tfrac13, \tfrac13)\approx(1.667, 0.333, 0.333)\) — converging there ✓
Problem 3 Linear Approximation & IFT — When Newton Fails 7 pts
Consider \(F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}x^2+y^2 \\ 2xy\end{pmatrix}\). We test Newton's method at two starting points.
  1. (a) Compute \([DF(x,y)]\) in general. Is \([DF(0,0)]\) invertible? What goes wrong if we try Newton's method at \((0,0)\)? 2 pts
  2. (b) State the condition from the Inverse Function Theorem that guarantees local invertibility of \(F\). Find all points where it fails for this \(F\). 3 pts
  3. (c) At \(\mathbf{x}_0=(2,1)\) compute one Newton step toward solving \(F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}\). (Hint: reformulate as \(G(x,y)=F(x,y)-\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}=\mathbf{0}\).) 2 pts
(a) Jacobian & Failure at (0,0)
\[[DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix}2x&2y\\2y&2x\end{pmatrix}, \qquad [DF(0,0)]=\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}.\] \(\det[DF(0,0)]=0\) — not invertible. Newton's step \(-[DF]^{-1}F\) is undefined (division by zero). Method cannot proceed from \((0,0)\).
(b) IFT Condition
The IFT requires \(\det[DF(\mathbf{x}_0)]\neq0\) — i.e., the Jacobian must be invertible. Here \(\det[DF(x,y)]=4x^2-4y^2=4(x-y)(x+y)\). This vanishes on the lines \(x=y\) and \(x=-y\).
IFT fails (and Newton may fail) at all \((x,y)\) with \(|x|=|y|\).
(c) Newton Step at (2,1)
\(G(x,y)=F(x,y)-\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}\), so \([DG]=[DF]\) (constant shift doesn't affect Jacobian). \(G(2,1)=\begin{pmatrix}5-5\\4-4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}\).
\(G(2,1)=\mathbf{0}\), so \((2,1)\) is already the exact solution — Newton terminates immediately.
Verify: \(F(2,1)=\begin{pmatrix}4+1\\4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\4\end{pmatrix}\) ✓
Problem 4 Polar Coordinates & Two-Step Iteration HARD 13 pts
Suppose we want to find the intersection of a spiral and a circle. Define
\[F(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}x^2+y^2-4 \\ x - y^2 + 1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{x}_0=(1,\,1).\]
  1. (a) Compute \([DF(1,1)]\) and explain why \(F\) is differentiable at \((1,1)\). 3 pts
  2. (b) Use linear approximation to estimate \(F(1.02,\,0.99)\). 2 pts
  3. (c) Express \(F\) in polar coordinates \(x=r\cos\theta,\,y=r\sin\theta\). Compute \(D_rF\) and \(D_\theta F\) at the point \((r,\theta)=(\sqrt{2}, \pi/4)\) (which corresponds to \((1,1)\)). 4 pts
  4. (d) Perform one Newton step from \((1,1)\) to find \(\mathbf{x}_1\). Then perform a second step from \(\mathbf{x}_1\) to find \(\mathbf{x}_2\). Is \(F(\mathbf{x}_2)\approx\mathbf{0}\)? 4 pts
(a) Jacobian at (1,1)
\[[DF(x,y)]=\begin{pmatrix}2x&2y\\1&-2y\end{pmatrix}, \qquad [DF(1,1)]=\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\1&-2\end{pmatrix}.\] All partial derivatives are polynomials — continuous everywhere — so \(F\) is differentiable at \((1,1)\).
(b) Linear Approximation
\(F(1,1)=\begin{pmatrix}1+1-4\\1-1+1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-2\\1\end{pmatrix}\). With \(\mathbf{h}=(0.02,-0.01)^T\): \[\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\1&-2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0.02\\-0.01\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0.02\\0.04\end{pmatrix}.\]
\[F(1.02,0.99)\approx\begin{pmatrix}-2\\1\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}0.02\\0.04\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-1.98\\1.04\end{pmatrix}.\]
(c) Polar Derivatives
Substitute \(x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta\): \[F=\begin{pmatrix}r^2-4 \\ r\cos\theta - r^2\sin^2\theta + 1\end{pmatrix}.\] \[D_rF=\begin{pmatrix}2r\\\cos\theta-2r\sin^2\theta\end{pmatrix}, \qquad D_\theta F=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-r\sin\theta-2r^2\sin\theta\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}.\] At \((r,\theta)=(\sqrt{2},\pi/4)\): \(\cos(\pi/4)=\sin(\pi/4)=1/\sqrt{2}\).
\[D_rF=\begin{pmatrix}2\sqrt{2}\\1/\sqrt{2}-2\sqrt{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\sqrt{2}\\1/\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{2}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\sqrt{2}\\-1/\sqrt{2}\end{pmatrix},\] \[D_\theta F=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-\sqrt{2}/\sqrt{2}-2\cdot2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-1-2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-3\end{pmatrix}.\]
(d) Two Newton Steps
Step 1: \(\det[DF(1,1)]=2(-2)-2(1)=-6.\) \[[DF(1,1)]^{-1}=\frac{1}{-6}\begin{pmatrix}-2&-2\\-1&2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/3&1/3\\1/6&-1/3\end{pmatrix}.\] \[[DF]^{-1}F(1,1)=\begin{pmatrix}1/3&1/3\\1/6&-1/3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}-2\\1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-1/3\\-2/3\end{pmatrix}.\]
\[\mathbf{x}_1=(1,1)-(-1/3,-2/3)=(4/3,\;5/3)\approx(1.333,\;1.667).\]
Step 2: \(F(4/3,5/3)=\begin{pmatrix}16/9+25/9-4\\4/3-25/9+1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}41/9-36/9\\12/9-25/9+9/9\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}5/9\\-4/9\end{pmatrix}.\) \([DF(4/3,5/3)]=\begin{pmatrix}8/3&10/3\\1&-10/3\end{pmatrix}\), \(\det=-80/9-10/3=-110/9.\) \([DF]^{-1}F\approx\frac{9}{-110}\begin{pmatrix}-10/3&-10/3\\-1&8/3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}5/9\\-4/9\end{pmatrix}=\frac{9}{-110}\begin{pmatrix}-50/27+40/27\\-5/9-32/27\end{pmatrix}\) \(=\frac{9}{-110}\begin{pmatrix}-10/27\\-47/27\end{pmatrix}\approx\begin{pmatrix}0.034\\0.143\end{pmatrix}.\)
\[\mathbf{x}_2\approx(1.333-0.034,\;1.667-0.143)\approx(1.299,\;1.524).\]
Check: \(F(1.299,1.524)\approx\begin{pmatrix}1.687+2.323-4\\1.299-2.323+1\end{pmatrix}\approx\begin{pmatrix}0.01\\-0.02\end{pmatrix}\approx\mathbf{0}\) ✓ Converging to a solution on the circle \(x^2+y^2=4\).
