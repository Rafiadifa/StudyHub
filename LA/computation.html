Chapter 3: Gaussian Elimination & Linear Systems

Recipe · 01 Row-reduce a matrix to RREF

1. Find the leftmost non-zero column. If the top entry is zero, swap rows.
2. Scale that row so the pivot is $1$.
3. Use row operations to clear every other entry in that column (both above and below).
4. Cover the pivot row, recurse on the submatrix below-and-right.
5. Stop when the submatrix is all zeros.

Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Reduce $ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $.

Step 1: Pivot is 1. Clear column 1 below it.
$ R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1 $ and $ R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_1 $.
Matrix becomes $ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $.

Step 2: Column 2 has no pivot. Move to column 3. Pivot is 3 in row 2.
Scale: $ R_2 \leftarrow R_2 / 3 \implies \text{Row 2 is } [0, 0, 1] $.

Step 3: Clear above and below the pivot in column 3.
$ R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2 $ (Row 3 becomes zeros).
$ R_1 \leftarrow R_1 + R_2 $.

Final RREF: $ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $. Rank is 2.
Type B: Conceptual Trap Premature Stopping: Don't stop at Row Echelon Form (REF) if asked for RREF! You must guarantee zeros above every leading 1, not just below.

Recipe · 02 Read off solutions to $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$

Augment to $[A\,|\,\mathbf{b}]$ and reduce.

1. If any row reads $[0\cdots0\,|\,c\ne0]$: inconsistent, no solution.
2. Otherwise set free variables to $0$ $\implies$ read off the particular solution $\mathbf{x}_p$ directly from the pivots.
3. For each free variable in turn, set it to 1 (others to 0) $\implies$ gives a basis vector of $\operatorname{Null}(A)$.
4. Full solution: $\mathbf{x}_p + \operatorname{span}\{\text{null-space basis}\}$.

Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Solve a system where the RREF of the augmented matrix is:
$ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 0 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right] $

Identify variables: Pivot columns are 1 and 3. $x_1$ and $x_3$ are bound. Column 2 has no pivot, so $x_2$ is a free variable.
Particular Solution ($\mathbf{x}_p$): Set free variable $x_2 = 0$. $\implies \mathbf{x}_p = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$.
Homogeneous Solution ($\mathbf{x}_h$): Set $x_2 = 1$, ignore the augmented column (treat it as 0).
$x_1 + 2(1) = 0 \implies x_1 = -2$. $\implies \mathbf{x}_h = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$.
Full Solution: $ \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $.

Chapter 4: Matrix Algebra & LU

Recipe · 01 LU & PLU Decomposition

1. Run elimination using only $R_i \leftarrow R_i - \ell_{ij}R_j$ (no swaps, no scaling).
2. Track every multiplier $\ell_{ij}$; place it in row $i$, col $j$ of $L$.
3. The resulting echelon matrix is $U$; fill $L$'s diagonal with 1s.
4. Verify: $LU = A$.

Type B: Conceptual Trap (PLU) Permuting the Past: If you perform a row swap, say swapping Row 2 and Row 3 after you have already calculated multipliers for Column 1, those multipliers MUST travel with their rows! The value previously stored in $\ell_{21}$ must move to $\ell_{31}$. Never leave an old multiplier in its original physical position when the row it belongs to is swapped.

Chapter 5: Bases, Coordinates, Rank

Recipe · 01 Find a basis for a span

Given vectors $v_1, \dots, v_k$:

  • Row Method: Place them as rows of a matrix $M$. Row-reduce $M$ to RREF. The non-zero rows form a basis.
  • Column Method: Place them as columns of a matrix $M$. Row-reduce $M$ to RREF to locate the pivots. The original columns in the pivot positions form a basis.
Type B: Conceptual Trap Row vs Column Divergence: If you use the row method, you can use the rows of the RREF directly as your basis. BUT if you use the column method, the columns of the RREF are fundamentally altered spaces! You must map the pivot positions back and grab the exact corresponding columns from the original matrix.
Recipe · 02 Build the change-of-basis matrix $I_{\mathcal{C}\leftarrow\mathcal{B}}$
$$ I_{\mathcal{C}\leftarrow\mathcal{B}} = \big[\, [b_1]_{\mathcal{C}} \mid [b_2]_{\mathcal{C}} \mid \cdots \mid [b_n]_{\mathcal{C}} \,\big] $$

1. For each vector $b_j$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$, solve $b_j = \sum_i c_i \cdot c_{\text{basis}_i}$ to find its coordinates in basis $\mathcal{C}$.
2. Stack these coordinate vectors as the columns of your transition matrix.

Chapter 6: Inner Products, Gram-Schmidt & Projections

★ Core Procedure Gram-Schmidt via Column-Shearing (Prof. Yang's Method)

This chapter emphasizes the unique column-shearing perspective. Do not just use the standard algebraic formula; understand how shearing naturally yields the $R$ matrix in the QR decomposition.

1. Compute $\|a_1\|^2 = a_1 \cdot a_1$ and the dot products $a_1 \cdot a_j$ for every $j>1$.
2. The ratios $r_{1j} = \frac{a_1 \cdot a_j}{a_1 \cdot a_1}$ are the shearing coefficients. They become row 1 of an upper triangular matrix $U$.
3. Shear: replace $a_j \leftarrow a_j - r_{1j}a_1$ for each $j>1$. Now all columns $j>1$ are orthogonal to column 1.
4. Move on to column 2 and repeat.

Type B: Conceptual Trap Premature Normalization: Never normalize to unit length during the shearing steps! If you divide by norms early, you introduce hideous square roots into your arithmetic, destroying your ability to calculate the next dot products cleanly. Keep matrices rational until the final $QR$ extraction.
Recipe · 03 Orthogonal projection & Least squares
$$ \operatorname{proj}_{\operatorname{Col}(A)}(\mathbf{b}) = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \mathbf{b} $$

Project a vector $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\operatorname{Col}(A)$:
1. Form the projection matrix $P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$. Check $P^2=P$ and $P^T=P$.
2. Projection of $\mathbf{b}$ is $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = P\mathbf{b}$.
3. Residual: $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} - \hat{\mathbf{b}}$; verify $\mathbf{r} \perp \operatorname{Col}(A)$ (i.e., $A^T\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$).

Chapter 7: Determinants

Recipe · 01 Cofactor Expansion & Core Properties
$$ \det(A) = \sum_{j=1}^n (-1)^{i+j} a_{ij} \det(M_{ij}) $$

Pick a row or column with many zeros. For each non-zero entry $a_{ij}$, multiply by the checkerboard sign $(-1)^{i+j}$ and the determinant of the minor $M_{ij}$ (the matrix formed by crossing out row $i$ and column $j$).

Fundamental Properties to Memorize
  • Multiplicativity: $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$
  • Transpose: $\det(A^T) = \det(A)$
  • Inverse: $\det(A^{-1}) = \frac{1}{\det(A)}$
  • Scaling: For an $n \times n$ matrix, $\det(cA) = c^n \det(A)$
Formulae · 02 Cramer's Rule & Adjugate Matrix

If $\det(A) \neq 0$, the unique solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ can be calculated component-by-component:

$$ x_i = \frac{\det(A_i(\mathbf{b}))}{\det(A)} $$

Where $A_i(\mathbf{b})$ is the matrix $A$ with its $i$-th column replaced by vector $\mathbf{b}$. Furthermore, the exact inverse of $A$ can be computed using the adjugate (the transpose of the cofactor matrix):

$$ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \operatorname{adj}(A) $$

Chapter 8: Eigenstuff, Spectral Theorem & SVD

Recipe · 01 Characteristic Equation & Diagonalization

To find the eigenvalues $\lambda$, solve the characteristic equation:

$$ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 $$

For each eigenvalue $\lambda$, find the basis for its eigenspace: $E_\lambda = \operatorname{Null}(A - \lambda I)$.

If an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors, it can be factored:

$$ A = P D P^{-1} $$
The Spectral Theorem If $A$ is symmetric ($A^T = A$), it is orthogonally diagonalizable. The eigenvectors can be chosen to be mutually orthogonal, meaning $P$ becomes an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (where $Q^{-1} = Q^T$): $$ A = Q D Q^T $$
★ Mastery · 02 Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
$$ A = U \Sigma V^T $$

For any $m \times n$ matrix $A$:

1. Form $A^TA$ ($n \times n$, symmetric positive semi-definite).
2. Find its eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \dots \ge \lambda_n \ge 0$. The singular values are $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$. Form $\Sigma$.
3. Find orthonormal eigenvectors of $A^TA$ — these become the columns of $V$.
4. For each $\sigma_i > 0$: compute columns of $U$ via $\mathbf{u}_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}A\mathbf{v}_i$.
5. Extend $U$ to an orthonormal basis if needed.

Truncated Error (Frobenius Norm) If you approximate $A$ using only the first $k$ singular values (PCA/compression), the error is the square root of the sum of the discarded squared singular values: $$ \|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i>k} \sigma_i^2} $$
