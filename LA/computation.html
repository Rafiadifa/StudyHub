Chapter 3: Gaussian Elimination & Linear Systems
Row-Reducing to RREF

1. Find the leftmost non-zero column. Swap rows if the top entry is zero.
2. Scale that row so the pivot is 1.
3. Use row operations to clear every other entry in that column (above and below).
4. Cover the pivot row, recurse on the submatrix below-and-right.

Conceptual Trap Premature Stopping: Don't stop at Row Echelon Form (REF) if asked for RREF! You must guarantee zeros above every leading 1.

Chapter 4: Matrix Algebra & LU Decomposition
LU & PLU Decomposition

1. Run elimination using only $R_i \to R_i - \ell_{ij}R_j$ (no swaps, no scaling).
2. Track every multiplier $\ell_{ij}$ and place it in row $i$, col $j$ of $L$.
3. The resulting echelon matrix is $U$; fill $L$'s diagonal with 1s.
4. If a zero pivot is encountered, row swap and record the permutation in $P$. Move previously calculated multipliers along with their rows!

Chapter 5: Bases, Coordinates & Rank
Finding a Basis for a Span

Given vectors $v_1, \dots, v_k$:

  • Row Method: Place vectors as rows. Row-reduce to RREF. The non-zero rows form a basis.
  • Column Method: Place vectors as columns. Row-reduce to find pivot positions. Grab the original columns from those pivot positions.
Change-of-Basis Matrix
$$ I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B} = \big[\, [b_1]_{\mathcal C} \mid [b_2]_{\mathcal C} \mid \cdots \mid [b_n]_{\mathcal C} \,\big] $$

To find the matrix converting from basis $\mathcal B$ to $\mathcal C$, express each vector of $\mathcal B$ as a linear combination of $\mathcal C$'s vectors, and stack the resulting coefficient vectors as columns.

Chapter 6: Inner Products & Orthogonality
Gram-Schmidt (Column-Shearing to QR)

Prof. Yang's specific method for preserving clean arithmetic:

1. Compute $\|a_1\|^2$ and dot products $a_1 \cdot a_j$ for $j>1$.
2. The ratios $r_{1j} = \frac{a_1 \cdot a_j}{a_1 \cdot a_1}$ become row 1 of an upper triangular matrix $U$.
3. Shear: $a_j \leftarrow a_j - r_{1j}a_1$. Now columns $j>1$ are orthogonal to column 1.
4. Repeat for column 2. Never normalize until the very end.

Orthogonal Projection
$$ \operatorname{proj}_{\operatorname{Col}(A)}(\mathbf{b}) = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T \mathbf{b} $$

Chapter 7: Determinants
1. Cofactor Expansion
$$ \det(A) = \sum_{j=1}^n (-1)^{i+j} a_{ij} \det(M_{ij}) $$

Pick a row or column with the most zeros. For each non-zero entry, cross out its row and column to form the minor $M_{ij}$. Multiply the determinant of the minor by the entry and the checkerboard sign.

2. Fundamental Properties
  • Multiplicativity: $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$
  • Transpose: $\det(A^T) = \det(A)$
  • Inverse: $\det(A^{-1}) = \frac{1}{\det(A)}$
  • Scaling: For an $n \times n$ matrix, $\det(cA) = c^n \det(A)$
3. Cramer's Rule & Adjugate Matrix

If $\det(A) \neq 0$, the unique solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ can be found component-by-component:

$$ x_i = \frac{\det(A_i(\mathbf{b}))}{\det(A)} $$

Where $A_i(\mathbf{b})$ is the matrix $A$ with its $i$-th column replaced by vector $\mathbf{b}$. Furthermore, the exact inverse of $A$ can be computed using the adjugate (the transpose of the cofactor matrix):

$$ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \operatorname{adj}(A) $$
Chapter 8: Eigenstuff, Spectral Theorem & SVD
1. Characteristic Equation & Eigenspaces

To find the eigenvalues $\lambda$, solve the characteristic equation:

$$ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 $$

For each eigenvalue $\lambda$, its corresponding eigenvectors form a basis for the eigenspace, found by computing the null space:

$$ E_\lambda = \operatorname{Null}(A - \lambda I) $$
2. Diagonalization

If an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors, it is diagonalizable:

$$ A = P D P^{-1} $$

Where $P$ contains the eigenvectors as columns, and $D$ is a diagonal matrix of the corresponding eigenvalues.

The Spectral Theorem If $A$ is symmetric ($A^T = A$), it is orthogonally diagonalizable. The eigenvectors can be chosen to be mutually orthogonal, meaning $P$ becomes an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (where $Q^{-1} = Q^T$): $$ A = Q D Q^T $$
3. Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
$$ A = U \Sigma V^T $$

1. Form $A^TA$ and find its eigenvalues $\lambda_i$.
2. Sort them descending. Singular values are $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$. Form $\Sigma$.
3. Find orthonormal eigenvectors of $A^TA$ to form the columns of $V$.
4. For $\sigma_i > 0$, compute columns of $U$ via $\mathbf{u}_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}A\mathbf{v}_i$. Extend to an ONB if needed.
