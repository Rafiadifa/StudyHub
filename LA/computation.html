Chapter 3 · Computational

Gaussian Elimination Recipes

Time2–4 min per 3×3
Recipe · 01 Row-reduce a matrix to RREF
1. Find the leftmost non-zero column. If the top entry is zero, swap rows. 2. Scale that row so the pivot is \(1\). 3. Use row 3 ops to clear every other entry in that column (both above and below). 4. Cover the pivot row, recurse on the submatrix below-and-right. 5. Stop when the submatrix is all zeros.
Cross-check: count pivots — that's the rank.
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Reduce \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 1: Pivot is 1. Clear column 1 below it.
\( R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1 \) and \( R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_1 \).
Matrix becomes \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 2: Column 2 has no pivot (entries are 0). Move to column 3. Pivot is 3 in row 2.
Scale: \( R_2 \leftarrow R_2 / 3 \implies \text{Row 2 is } [0, 0, 1] \).

Step 3: Clear above and below the pivot in column 3.
\( R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2 \) (Row 3 becomes zeros).
\( R_1 \leftarrow R_1 + R_2 \).

Final RREF: \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \). Rank is 2.
Type B: Conceptual Trap Premature Stopping: Don't stop at Row Echelon Form (REF) if asked for RREF! You must guarantee zeros above every leading 1, not just below.

Recipe · 02 Read off solutions to \(A\mathbf x=\mathbf b\)
Augment to \([A\,|\,\mathbf b]\) and reduce. 1. If any row reads \([0\cdots0\,|\,c\ne0]\): inconsistent, no solution. 2. Otherwise set free variables to \(0\) → read off the particular solution \(\mathbf x_p\) directly from the pivots. 3. For each free variable in turn, set it to 1 (others to 0) → gives a basis vector of \(\operatorname{Null}(A)\). 4. Full solution: \(\mathbf x_p+\operatorname{span}\{\text{null-space basis}\}\).
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Solve a system where the RREF of the augmented matrix is:
\( \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 0 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right] \)

Identify variables: Pivot columns are 1 and 3. \(x_1\) and \(x_3\) are bound. Column 2 has no pivot, so \(x_2\) is a free variable.
Equations:
\( x_1 + 2x_2 = 4 \implies x_1 = 4 - 2x_2 \)
\( x_3 = -1 \)
Particular Solution (\(x_p\)): Set free variable \(x_2 = 0\).
\( \mathbf{x}_p = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \).
Homogeneous Solution (\(x_h\)): Set \(x_2 = 1\), ignore the augmented column (treat it as 0).
\( \mathbf{x}_h = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \).
Full Solution: \( \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \).
Apply · 03 Test invertibility of a square \(A\)
Reduce \(A\) to RREF. Invertible \(\iff\) RREF \(=I\) \(\iff\) \(n\) pivots \(\iff\) \(\det(A)\ne0\) \(\iff\) \(\operatorname{Null}(A)=\{0\}\). To compute \(A^{-1}\) directly: row-reduce \([A\,|\,I]\) to \([I\,|\,A^{-1}]\).
Type B: Conceptual Trap Non-Square Matrices: Only square matrices can be truly invertible. If a matrix is \(3 \times 4\), it might have a right-inverse, but it can never be tested for strict invertibility or determinant. Always check dimensions first.

Matrix Algebra & LU

Time5–8 min for a 3×3 LU
Recipe · 01 LU decomposition by hand (no pivoting)
1. Run elimination using only "\(R_i\to R_i-\ell_{ij}R_j\)" (no swaps, no scaling). 2. Track every multiplier \(\ell_{ij}\); place it in row \(i\), col \(j\) of \(L\). 3. The resulting echelon matrix is \(U\); fill \(L\)'s diagonal with \(1\)s. 4. Verify: \(LU=A\).
If any pivot lands on zero, a swap is required → see PLU below.
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Factor \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 4 & 5 & 3 \\ -2 & 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 1: Clear below \(A_{11}=2\).
\( R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - (2)R_1 \implies \ell_{21} = 2 \). Matrix is \( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 1 \\ -2 & 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \).
\( R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - (-1)R_1 \implies \ell_{31} = -1 \). Matrix is \( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 2: Clear below \(A_{22}=3\).
\( R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - (1)R_2 \implies \ell_{32} = 1 \).
Matrix is now upper triangular \( U = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 3: Assemble \(L\) with 1s on the diagonal and \(\ell_{ij}\) in the lower triangle.
\( L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).
Recipe · 02 PLU when pivoting is needed
1. Whenever a pivot is zero, swap with a lower row whose entry is non-zero. 2. Record the swap as a permutation matrix \(P\) (each swap left-multiplies the previous \(P\)). 3. Continue elimination; the multipliers fill \(L\) using the post-swap row indices. 4. Result: \(PA=LU\). Verify by computing both sides.
Type B: Conceptual Trap Permuting the Past: If you perform a row swap, say swapping Row 2 and Row 3 after you have already calculated multipliers for Column 1, those multipliers MUST travel with their rows! The value previously stored in \(\ell_{21}\) must move to \(\ell_{31}\). Never leave an old multiplier in its original physical position when the row it belongs to is swapped.
Apply · 03 Matrix Transformations & Combinations

Chapter 5 · Computational

Bases, Coordinates, Rank

Time5–10 min per problem
Recipe · 01 Find a basis for a span
Given vectors \(v_1,\dots,v_k\): 1. Place them as rows of a matrix \(M\). 2. Row-reduce \(M\) to RREF. 3. The non-zero rows of RREF form a basis of \(\operatorname{span}\{v_i\}\). The number of non-zero rows is the dimension.
Alternative (column method): place as columns, reduce, and take the original columns in the pivot positions.
Type B: Conceptual Trap Row vs Column Divergence: If you use the row method, you can use the rows of the RREF directly as your basis. BUT if you use the column method, the columns of the RREF are fundamentally altered spaces! You must map the pivot positions back and grab the exact corresponding columns from the original matrix.
Recipe · 03 Build the change-of-basis matrix \(I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\)
Columns of \(I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B}\) are the \(\mathcal B\)-basis vectors written in \(\mathcal C\)-coordinates.
1. For each \(b_j\), solve \(b_j=\sum_i [b_j]_{\mathcal C,i}\,c_i\) to find the \(\mathcal C\)-coordinates. 2. Stack these as columns.
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Find the change of basis matrix \( I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B} \) where \( \mathcal B = \{(1,1), (0,1)\} \) and \( \mathcal C = \{(2,0), (1,1)\} \).

Express each basis vector of \(\mathcal B\) as a linear combination of \(\mathcal C\):
\( b_1 = (1,1) = 0 \cdot (2,0) + 1 \cdot (1,1) \implies [b_1]_{\mathcal C} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \).
\( b_2 = (0,1) = (-1/2) \cdot (2,0) + 1 \cdot (1,1) \implies [b_2]_{\mathcal C} = \begin{bmatrix} -1/2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \).

Stack as columns: \( I_{\mathcal C\leftarrow\mathcal B} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1/2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).

Inner Products, Cholesky, Gram-Schmidt

Time10–15 min for full QR
Professor Yang's Signature Content: This chapter emphasizes the unique column-shearing perspective of Gram-Schmidt. Do not just use the standard algebraic formula; understand how shearing naturally yields the \(R\) matrix in the QR decomposition.
★ Apply · 03 Gram-Schmidt via column-shearing (Prof. Yang's procedure → QR)
1. Compute \(\mathbf a_1\cdot\mathbf a_1\) (= \(\|\mathbf a_1\|^2\)) and the dot products \(\mathbf a_1\cdot\mathbf a_j\) for every \(j>1\). 2. The ratios \(r_{1j}=\dfrac{\mathbf a_1\cdot\mathbf a_j}{\mathbf a_1\cdot\mathbf a_1}\) are the shearing coefficients. They become row 1 of an upper triangular matrix \(U\) to the right. 3. Shear: replace \(\mathbf a_j\to\mathbf a_j-r_{1j}\mathbf a_1\) for each \(j>1\). Now all columns \(j>1\) are orthogonal to column 1. 4. Move on to column 2 and repeat.
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Let \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \). Call columns \( a_1, a_2, a_3 \).

Pass 1 (Using \(a_1\)):
\( \|a_1\|^2 = 4 \).
\( a_1 \cdot a_2 = 2 \implies r_{12} = 2/4 = 1/2 \).
\( a_2' = a_2 - \frac{1}{2}a_1 = [1/2, 1/2, -1/2, -1/2]^T \).

\( a_1 \cdot a_3 = 2 \implies r_{13} = 2/4 = 1/2 \).
\( a_3' = a_3 - \frac{1}{2}a_1 = [1/2, -1/2, -1/2, 1/2]^T \).

Pass 2 (Using \(a_2'\)):
\( \|a_2'\|^2 = (1/4)*4 = 1 \).
\( a_2' \cdot a_3' = 1/4 - 1/4 + 1/4 - 1/4 = 0 \implies r_{23} = 0/1 = 0 \).
\( a_3'' = a_3' - 0(a_2') = [1/2, -1/2, -1/2, 1/2]^T \).

Assembly:
Orthogonal (unnormalized) matrix \( W = [a_1, a_2', a_3''] \).
Upper triangular matrix of ratios \( U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).
Notice \(A = WU\). To get standard \(QR\), normalize \(W\) into \(Q\) and scale \(U\) by the lengths to become \(R\).
Type B: Conceptual Trap Premature Normalization: Never normalize to unit length during the shearing steps! If you divide by norms early, you introduce hideous square roots into your arithmetic, destroying your ability to calculate the next dot products cleanly. Keep \(W\) and \(U\) rational until the final extraction.
Recipe · 05 Orthogonal projection & least squares
Project a vector \(\mathbf b\) onto \(\operatorname{Col}(A)\): 1. Form the projection matrix \(P=A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\). Check \(P^2=P\) and \(P^T=P\). 2. Projection of \(\mathbf b\) is \(\hat{\mathbf b}=P\mathbf b\). 3. Residual: \(\mathbf r=\mathbf b-\hat{\mathbf b}\); verify \(\mathbf r\perp\operatorname{Col}(A)\) (i.e., \(A^T\mathbf r=\mathbf 0\)).

Determinants

Time1–3 min for 3×3
Recipe · 01 Cofactor expansion (\(n\times n\))
Pick a row or column with many zeros. For each non-zero entry \(a_{ij}\): 1. Delete row \(i\) and column \(j\) — leaves a \((n-1)\times(n-1)\) minor \(M_{ij}\). 2. Sign: \((-1)^{i+j}\) (checkerboard). 3. \(\det(A)=\sum_{j}(-1)^{i+j}a_{ij}\det(M_{ij})\).
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Calculate \( \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 3 & -1 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \).

Expand along row 3 (it has the most zeros).
Only the '5' is non-zero. Its position is (3,3), so sign is \( (-1)^{3+3} = +1 \).
\( \det = 5 \cdot \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \).
\( \det = 5 \cdot (2(-1) - 0) = -10 \).

Eigenstuff, Spectral, SVD

Time15–25 min for full SVD
★ Apply · 05 Singular Value Decomposition step-by-step
For any \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\), compute \(A=U\Sigma V^T\): 1. Form \(A^TA\) (\(n\times n\), symmetric PSD). 2. Find its eigenvalues \(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\cdots\ge\lambda_n\ge 0\). The singular values are \(\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i}\). 3. Find orthonormal eigenvectors of \(A^TA\) — these become the columns of \(V\). 4. For each \(\sigma_i>0\): set \(\mathbf u_i=A\mathbf v_i/\sigma_i\). 5. Extend \(U\) to an ONB if needed.
Type A: Numerical Walkthrough Compute SVD for \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 1: \( A^TA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 2: Eigenvalues of \(A^TA\): \(\lambda_1=3, \lambda_2=2\).
Singular values: \(\sigma_1=\sqrt{3}, \sigma_2=\sqrt{2}\).
\( \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{3} & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{2} \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 3: Eigenvectors for \(A^TA\) (must match order of \(\lambda\)):
For \(\lambda_1=3\): \(v_1 = [0, 1]^T\). For \(\lambda_2=2\): \(v_2 = [1, 0]^T\).
\( V = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \).

Step 4: Active columns of \(U\):
\( u_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}Av_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}[1, 1, 1]^T \).
\( u_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}Av_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1, 0, -1]^T \).

Step 5: Extend \(U\): need \(u_3\) orthogonal to \(u_1, u_2\). Cross product yields \([-1, 2, -1]^T\). Normalize to \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}[-1, 2, -1]^T\). Assemble \(U\).
Type B: Conceptual Trap SVD Strict Ordering: You MUST order singular values in descending order (\(\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \dots\)) and match the columns of \(V\) and \(U\) identically to this sorted sequence. Failing to sort them destroys the low-rank approximation property of SVD.
Recipe · 07 Classify a quadratic form
Given \(q(\mathbf x)=\mathbf x^TA\mathbf x\) (symmetrise \(A\) if needed: \(A\leftarrow(A+A^T)/2\)): 1. Compute the eigenvalues of \(A\). 2. Classify by sign pattern: all \(>0\) → positive definite (ellipsoid); all \(<0\) → negative definite; mix of \(\pm\) → indefinite (saddle / hyperboloid).
