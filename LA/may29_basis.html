Basis & Span Drill

Final · 25 Jun Week 1 of 6
Quick reference. A basis of a subspace \(V\subseteq\mathbb R^n\) is a linearly independent spanning set; \(\dim V\) is the (unique) number of vectors in any basis. For the coordinate map \([\,\cdot\,]_B:V\to\mathbb R^{\dim V}\), independence and spanning transfer along the isomorphism, so testing the coordinate vectors in \(\mathbb R^n\) settles the question in any abstract space (\(P_n\), \(M_{m\times n}\), function spaces…).
Problem 1 Span & independence in \(\mathbb R^4\) 10 pts
Decide what subspace of \(\mathbb R^4\) is spanned by four given vectors, and locate a specific vector inside it.
\[v_1=(1,2,0,1),\quad v_2=(2,4,1,3),\quad v_3=(3,6,1,4),\quad v_4=(0,0,1,1).\]
  1. (a) Do \(v_1,\dots,v_4\) span all of \(\mathbb R^4\)? [2 pts]
  2. (b) Find a basis for \(\operatorname{span}\{v_1,\dots,v_4\}\) and state its dimension. [3 pts]
  3. (c) Are the four vectors linearly independent? If not, write each redundant vector as a combination of the others. [2 pts]
  4. (d) Is \(w=(1,2,2,3)\) in the span? [2 pts]
  5. (e) Give the coordinates of \(w\) in your basis from (b). [1 pt]
Row-reduce with the \(v_i\) as rows: \(R_2-2R_1=R_3-3R_1=R_4=(0,0,1,1)\), so only two pivots survive.
No — rank \(=2<4\), so they cannot span \(\mathbb R^4\).
The two distinct rows of the reduction are \((1,2,0,1)\) and \((0,0,1,1)\), which are the columns of the original first and fourth vectors.
Basis \(\{(1,2,0,1),\,(0,0,1,1)\}\); \(\dim=2\).
Dependent. From the reduction \(v_2-2v_1=v_4\) and \(v_3-3v_1=v_4\).
\(v_2=2v_1+v_4,\quad v_3=3v_1+v_4\) (and \(v_3-v_2=v_1\)).
Solve \(w=a(1,2,0,1)+b(0,0,1,1)\): \(a=1\) (first entry), \(b=2\) (third), check second \(2a=2\)✓ and fourth \(a+b=3\)✓.
Yes — \(w=v_1+2v_4\).
Direct from (d).
\([w]_{\{v_1,v_4\}}=(1,2)\).
Problem 2 Basis of a sensor-network null space 10 pts
A small sensor mesh has three consistency constraints; the valid sensor-state vectors are exactly the null space of the matrix below. Describe that space completely.
\[A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-1&3\\2&4&-1&7\\1&2&0&4\end{pmatrix}.\]
  1. (a) Row-reduce \(A\) to RREF; identify pivot and free variables. [3 pts]
  2. (b) Find a basis for \(\operatorname{Null}(A)\) and its nullity. [3 pts]
  3. (c) Give \(\operatorname{rank}(A)\). [2 pts]
  4. (d) Verify rank-nullity bookkeeping for this matrix. [2 pts]
\(R_2-2R_1=(0,0,1,1)\); \(R_3-R_1=(0,0,1,1)\); clearing the \(-1\) in row 1 via \(R_1+R_2'\):
RREF \(=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&0&4\\0&0&1&1\\0&0&0&0\end{pmatrix}\). Pivots: \(x_1,x_3\). Free: \(x_2,x_4\).
\(x_1=-2x_2-4x_4,\;x_3=-x_4\). Setting \((x_2,x_4)=(1,0)\) and \((0,1)\):
\(\operatorname{Null}(A)=\operatorname{span}\{(-2,1,0,0),\;(-4,0,-1,1)\},\quad \text{nullity}=2.\)
Two pivots in RREF.
\(\operatorname{rank}(A)=2\).
\(\operatorname{rank}+\text{nullity}=2+2=4=\) number of columns of \(A\). \(\checkmark\)
Bookkeeping confirmed: \(2+2=4\).
Problem 3 Coordinate map in \(P_2\) 9 pts
A colleague proposes the set \(S=\big(1+x,\;x+x^2,\;1+x^2\big)\) as a "nicer" basis of the polynomial space \(P_2\) (polynomials of degree \(\le 2\)).
  1. (a) Decide whether \(S\) is a basis of \(P_2\). [4 pts]
  2. (b) Find the coordinate vector of \(p(x)=2+4x+4x^2\) relative to \(S\). [3 pts]
  3. (c) In one or two sentences, explain why checking independence of the three coordinate vectors in \(\mathbb R^3\) settles the question. [2 pts]
In the standard basis \((1,x,x^2)\) the three polynomials have coordinate vectors \((1,1,0),(0,1,1),(1,0,1)\). The determinant of the matrix with these as columns is \(\det\!\begin{pmatrix}1&0&1\\1&1&0\\0&1&1\end{pmatrix}=2\neq 0\), so they are independent. Three independent vectors in the 3-dimensional space \(P_2\) form a basis.
Yes — \(S\) is a basis of \(P_2\).
Solve \(a(1+x)+b(x+x^2)+c(1+x^2)=2+4x+4x^2\): constant \(a+c=2\), \(x\)-term \(a+b=4\), \(x^2\)-term \(b+c=4\). Subtract: \(b-c=2\), with \(b+c=4\Rightarrow b=3,c=1,a=1\). Check \(1(1+x)+3(x+x^2)+1(1+x^2)=2+4x+4x^2\).\(\checkmark\)
\([p]_S=(1,3,1)\).
The coordinate map \([\,\cdot\,]_{\text{std}}:P_2\to\mathbb R^3\) is an isomorphism of vector spaces; it sends bases to bases and preserves independence and spanning.
So \(S\) is a basis of \(P_2\) iff its coordinate images form a basis of \(\mathbb R^3\).
Problem 4 Subspaces of \(M_{2\times 2}\) — stress tensors 10 pts
A stress engineer models 2-D state with \(2\times2\) matrices. Two natural subspaces appear: \(W\) = symmetric matrices (physical stress is symmetric), and \(U\) = trace-zero matrices (the deviatoric, volume-preserving part).
  1. (a) Show \(W\) is a subspace of \(M_{2\times2}\). [2 pts]
  2. (b) Give a basis and the dimension of \(W\). [3 pts]
  3. (c) Give the dimension of \(U\). [2 pts]
  4. (d) Find \(\dim(W\cap U)\) and then \(\dim(W+U)\) via the dimension formula. What space is \(W+U\)? [3 pts]
\(0^{\mathsf T}=0\); if \(A=A^{\mathsf T}\), \(B=B^{\mathsf T}\) and \(c\in\mathbb R\) then \((A+cB)^{\mathsf T}=A^{\mathsf T}+cB^{\mathsf T}=A+cB\).
\(W\) contains \(0\) and is closed under \(+\) and scaling \(\Rightarrow\) subspace.
Every symmetric \(2\times2\) matrix has the form \(\begin{psmallmatrix}a&b\\b&d\end{psmallmatrix}= a\begin{psmallmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{psmallmatrix}+ d\begin{psmallmatrix}0&0\\0&1\end{psmallmatrix}+ b\begin{psmallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{psmallmatrix}\).
Basis \(\Big\{\!\begin{psmallmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{psmallmatrix}, \begin{psmallmatrix}0&0\\0&1\end{psmallmatrix}, \begin{psmallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{psmallmatrix}\!\Big\},\quad\dim W=3.\)
\(U\) is cut out of \(M_{2\times2}\) (dim 4) by a single linear equation \(a+d=0\), so \(\dim U=4-1=3\).
\(\dim U=3\).
\(W\cap U=\) symmetric \emph{and} trace-zero \(=\begin{psmallmatrix}a&b\\b&-a\end{psmallmatrix}\), two free parameters, so \(\dim(W\cap U)=2\). By the dimension formula \(\dim(W+U)=\dim W+\dim U-\dim(W\cap U)=3+3-2=4\).
\(\dim(W\cap U)=2,\quad \dim(W+U)=4=\dim M_{2\times2}\Rightarrow W+U=M_{2\times2}\) (everything).
Problem 5 The four fundamental subspaces of \(A\) 11 pts
For the matrix below, locate bases of the column space, row space, null space, and left null space, and check rank-nullity.
\[A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&1&3\\2&4&3&8\\1&2&2&5\end{pmatrix}.\]
  1. (a) Basis for \(\operatorname{Col}(A)\) and its dimension. [3 pts]
  2. (b) Basis for \(\operatorname{Row}(A)\). [2 pts]
  3. (c) Basis for \(\operatorname{Null}(A)\). [3 pts]
  4. (d) Verify rank-nullity and confirm \(\dim\operatorname{Col}=\dim\operatorname{Row}\). [2 pts]
  5. (e) Basis for the left null space \(\{\mathbf y:\mathbf y^{\mathsf T}A=\mathbf 0\}\). [1 pt]
Row-reduce: \(R_2-2R_1=(0,0,1,2)\), \(R_3-R_1=(0,0,1,2)\); clearing leaves RREF \(\begin{pmatrix}1&2&0&1\\0&0&1&2\\0&0&0&0\end{pmatrix}\), pivots in columns 1 and 3. Column space basis = pivot columns of the original \(A\): \((1,2,1)\) and \((1,3,2)\).
Basis \(\{(1,2,1),\,(1,3,2)\},\quad \dim\operatorname{Col}=2\).
Take the nonzero rows of the RREF.
Basis \(\{(1,2,0,1),\,(0,0,1,2)\},\quad \dim\operatorname{Row}=2\).
Free \(x_2,x_4\): \(x_1=-2x_2-x_4,\; x_3=-2x_4\). Set \((x_2,x_4)=(1,0)\) and \((0,1)\).
Basis \(\{(-2,1,0,0),\,(-1,0,-2,1)\},\quad \text{nullity}=2\).
\(\operatorname{rank}+\text{nullity}=2+2=4=\#\text{columns}\). \(\checkmark\) Also \(\dim\operatorname{Col}=\dim\operatorname{Row}=2\) — the row rank \(=\) column rank fact.
Bookkeeping confirmed. Row rank \(=\) column rank \(= 2\).
The rows satisfy \(R_3=R_2-R_1\), i.e. \(R_1-R_2+R_3=0\), so \(\mathbf y=(1,-1,1)\) annihilates the rows. Left-null dimension \(=m-\operatorname{rank}=3-2=1\).
Basis \(\{(1,-1,1)\}\) for the left null space, \(\dim=1\).
